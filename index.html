Tutor's Note: A Quick Tip on Consonant Sounds

The sound of some consonants changes slightly depending on their position. Don't worry, you'll get used to this naturally! Here's a quick guide:

  • ㄱ (g/k): Sounds like 'k' at the start of a word (가방 - kabang), but a softer 'g' between vowels (아기 - agi).
  • ㄷ (d/t): Sounds like 't' at the start of a word (다시 - tashi), but a softer 'd' between vowels (미디어 - midi-eo).
  • ㅂ (b/p): Sounds like 'p' at the start of a word (바보 - pabo), but a softer 'b' between vowels (나비 - nabi).

3. Syllable Blocks & Stroke Order

Read syllable blocks and write them using correct stroke order.

Understanding Syllable Blocks: Hangul characters are grouped into syllable blocks. Every block must have at least one consonant and one vowel.

Basic Structures:

  • **Consonant + Vowel (C+V):** 가, 나, 고, 노
  • **Consonant + Vowel + Consonant (C+V+C):** 강, 눈, 책
  • Silent Consonant + Vowel: If a syllable starts with a vowel sound, it must use the silent consonant 'ㅇ' as a placeholder. For example: ㅇ + ㅏ → 아 (a), ㅇ + ㅣ → 이 (i). This is why words like 아이 (child) start with ㅇ.

Stroke Order Examples

집
1. ㅈ (top to bottom, then left to right, then diagonal)
2. ㅣ (top to bottom)
3. ㅂ (top to bottom, then left to right, then top to bottom, then left to right - final consonant)
물
1. ㅁ (square shape, usually top to bottom, then left to right, then top to bottom, then left to right)
2. ㅜ (left to right, then top to bottom)
3. ㄹ (top to bottom, then left to right, then top to bottom, then left to right - final consonant)
책
1. ㅊ (top to bottom, then left to right, then diagonal, then small horizontal stroke)
2. ㅐ (top to bottom, then left to right, then top to bottom)
3. ㄱ (left to right, then down - final consonant)

General Stroke Order Rules:

  • Top to bottom
  • Left to right

4. Final Consonants (받침)

Understand how consonants work in the final position of a syllable.

What is Batchim (받침)?

Batchim refers to the final consonant(s) in a Korean syllable block. They are only pronounced in one of **seven representative sounds** when at the end of a word or before another consonant.

The 7 Representative Batchim Sounds

[ㄹ] (l)ㄹ말 (mal, horse/word) → [말]

A Note on Double Batchim (겹받침)

When a syllable has a double batchim, usually only one of the consonants is pronounced. Don't try to say both! Here's a quick rule of thumb:

  • Usually, the **first** consonant is pronounced. Ex: 앉다 (to sit) → [안따], 많다 (to be many) → [만타].
  • For ㄺ, ㄻ, ㄿ, the **second** consonant is pronounced. Ex: 읽다 (to read) → [익따], 젊다 (to be young) → [점따].

✦ Important: Spelling vs. Pronunciation

As we get into pronunciation rules, remember this key point: These changes affect spoken Korean but not how it’s written. The spelling of a word stays the same, even if it sounds different when you say it. Think of it like "knife" in English—you don't hear the 'k', but you still have to write it!

5. The #1 Pronunciation Rule: Linking (연음)

Learn the most common and important sound change for fluent reading.

When a syllable ending with a **batchim** is followed by a syllable starting with a **vowel (ㅇ)**, the batchim sound moves over to replace the silent 'ㅇ'. This makes the words flow together smoothly.

한국어 (Han-guk-eo) → [한구거]
The ㄱ (k) from 국 moves to the next syllable.
음악을 (eum-ak-eul) → [으마글]
The ㄱ (k) from 악 moves to the next syllable.
옷이 (ot-i) → [오시]
The ㅅ (s) from 옷 moves over and keeps its 's' sound.

6. Advanced Pronunciation Rules (For Reference)

You've mastered the basics! The following rules will help you sound more like a native speaker. Don't worry about memorizing them all at once. Use this section as a reference as you encounter these sounds in new words.

1. Aspiration (격음화)

When a plain consonant (ㄱ, ㄷ, ㅂ, ㅈ) meets ㅎ, they combine to form an aspirated (stronger) consonant (ㅋ, ㅌ, ㅍ, ㅊ).

ㄱ + ㅎ → ㅋ좋다 (jo-ta)[조타]
ㄷ + ㅎ → ㅌ닫히다 (dat-hi-da)[다치다]
ㅂ + ㅎ → ㅍ입학 (ip-hak)[이팍]
ㅈ + ㅎ → ㅊ어떻게 (eo-tteo-ke)[어떠케]

2. Palatalization (구개음화)

When a batchim ㄷ or ㅌ is followed by a syllable starting with the vowel 이, they change to a ㅈ and ㅊ sound, respectively.

ㄷ + 이 → [지]해돋이 (hae-dod-i)[해도지]
ㅌ + 이 → [치]붙이다 (but-i-da)[부치다]

3. Nasalization (비음화)

When a stop consonant (ㅂ, ㄷ, ㄱ) is followed by a nasal consonant (ㄴ, ㅁ), the stop consonant changes to its nasal equivalent (ㅁ, ㄴ, ㅇ).

ㅂ/ㅍ + ㄴ/ㅁ → [ㅁ]입니다 (ip-ni-da)[임니다]
ㄷ/ㅅ/ㅈ/ㅊ/ㅌ + ㄴ/ㅁ → [ㄴ]있는 (it-neun)[인는]
ㄱ/ㅋ/ㄲ + ㄴ/ㅁ → [ㅇ]한국말 (han-guk-mal)[한궁말]

4. Lateralization (유음화)

When ㄴ meets ㄹ (or vice versa), they both change to a liquid ㄹ (l) sound.

ㄴ + ㄹ → ㄹㄹ신라 (sin-ra)[실라]
ㄹ + ㄴ → ㄹㄹ설날 (seol-nal)[설랄]

5. Tensification / Hardening (경음화)

A plain consonant becomes a tense (double) consonant after certain batchim.

ㄱ/ㄷ/ㅂ + ㄱ/ㄷ/ㅂ/ㅅ/ㅈ → ㄲ/ㄸ/ㅃ/ㅆ/ㅉ학교 (hak-gyo)[학꾜]
